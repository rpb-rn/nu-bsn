Stress

Physiological and Psychological Response to Stress

Stress

  • biological and psychological response to a perceived or experienced stressor
  • can be positive or maladaptive
  • survival response - affects a lot of systems
  • Chronic Stress - repeated exposure, no opportunity for recovery

Categories:
Psychological - loss of self-esteem, grief, fear, cognitive, loss of personal security
Physiological - injury, heat/cold, illness, pain, substance abuse, etc

Pathophysiology of Stress

  • amygdala sends "distress call" to hypothalamus, which releases CRH -> pituitary release adrenocorticotropin hormone (ACTH)
  • Adrenal gland -> cortisol, epinephrine

Fight-or-Flight Response -> can be fight, faint, flight, freeze, fawn

General Adaptation Syndrome - GAS

  • when an event or stressor threatens the body and activates a three-stage response

Stages of Gas:

  1. Alarm Stage
    • aka, alarm stage => Fight-Flight-Freeze-Faint-or-Fawn
    • initial, brief, and adaptive response
    • sympathetic nervous system is activated: shock, then counter shock
    • shock -> decrease in blood pressure, temperature, muscle tone, and a loss of bodily fluid
    • countershock -> increased sympathetic nervous system (SNS) activity -> "fight response"
      • increased HR, BP RR, heighted cognitive processes, senses, glucose, endurance, decreased digestion (nonessential)
  2. Resistance Stage
    • aka adaptation stage => body attempts to adapt to the stressor
    • the stressor diminishes, the individual can return to homeostasis
  3. Exhaustion Stage
    • body has depleted its resources and can no longer adapt to the stressor
    • tissues and organs are depleted, and the result can lead to illness, disease, or death

Levels of Anxiety

  1. Mild
    • Triggered by stressor
    • Increased awareness, hypervigilance
    • peak problem-solving ability
    • Tension-relieving behaviors: nail-biting, foot-tapping, fidgeting
    • restlessness
  2. Moderate
    • Stress unresolved or perceived strongly
    • Narrowed perception field, decreased concentration
    • Limited learning ability, still problem-solves
    • Sympathetic activation → ↑vitals
    • Still has ability to learn and practice coping skills
  3. Severe
    • Ongoing or threatening stress
    • Distorted perception, focus on single detail
    • Minimal problem-solving, feels no solution
    • Symptoms: somatic complaints, impending doom, withdrawal, loud speech, nausea
  4. Panic
    • Unable to process environment
    • Disorganized, irrational, dissociation
    • Fight-flight-fawn-faint-freeze responses
    • Possible hallucinations, delusions
    • Counterpoint: stress -> depletion of energy and the breakdown of resilience mechanisms -> erosion of health

Good stress or bad stress?

  • research says stress is neither good or bad
  • Eustress -> positive stress response to a challenging but beneficial task that results in development growth, mastery, or a sense of fulfillment.
  • Viewing stress as positive - good adaptation

Causes & Effects

Acute Stress

  • sudden exposure to adverse stressor
  • can last for short period or a long time, depending on individual

Chronic Stress

  • prolonged exposure
  • can lead to: eating disorders, mood disorders, and substance use disorders, as well as physical diseases such as hypertension, heart disease, obesity, metabolic syndrome, arthritis, and type 2 diabetes.

Response to stress:
Acute Stress - ↑ reaction time, ↑ energy, hyperawareness, ↑HR
Chronic Stress - anxiety, ↑ infection, insomnia, depression

Toxic Stress

  • result of a prolonged or chronic stress response system
  • body learns that experiencing fear, stressors, or trauma is normal
  • biological and neurological changes to brain and body
  • example: chronic stress since childhood, chronic neglect, exposure to violence, economic hardship, caregiver substance abuse
  • referred to as ACEs -> adverse childhood experience

Mediators of Stress Response

Mediators => either Individual or Environmental

Individual Factors:

  • Perceptions & Attitudes
    • hardiness and resilience
    • lack of control, low self-esteem, low self-confidence, and a sense of hopelessness
  • Temperament & Personality
    • temperament - energy level, emotional responsiveness, demeanor, mood, response tempo, behavioral inhibition, and willingness to explore
    • personality traits - characteristics drawn from a pattern of behaviors, attitudes, feelings, and habits.
  • Genetics/Neurodevelopment - health condition, gender, prenatal care, age
  • Lifestyle
    • modifiable habits that can be related to individual choices and patterns of behaviors
    • use of drugs and alcohol, smoking, physical activity, sleep, and eating

Personality Traits:
Neuroticism vs. Emotional Stability
Extroversion vs. Introversion
Openness to Experience vs. Closeness to Experiences
Conscientiousness vs. Lack of Direction
Agreeableness vs. Antagonism

Hardiness -> based on a positive self-perception and attitudes of hopefulness

Environmental Factors:

  • Environment & Setting
    • Safe, secure, and positive school or work environments are considered protective environmental factors
    • high stress: pollution, abuse, or crime
  • Cultural
    • connection to one's culture is a form of social support, protective factor
    • cause of stress: adapting to new cultures => language, discrimination, stereotypes
  • Support System: family & community
    • Safe, supportive, and secure early relationships can help individuals self-regulate
    • Risk factor: lack of parental support and parent-child bonding
    • ages of 18 and 23 => decreased social interactions -> loneliness, stress
  • Spirituality & Religion
    • offer positive influence, feeling of community, develop resilience

Management

Coping - learning to adjust to the stress or stressor while trying to keep themselves in a state of mental and physical equilibrium

Holmes-Rahe Social Readjustment Scale

  • measures positive and negative life events and assigns a value to certain stressors
  • can provide insight into client’s experience of stress and risk for developing an illness
  • each life event => assign point

Coping Styles:

  • Problem-focused - adaptive
  • Emotion-Focused - adaptive; reduces negative emotions
  • Avoidant Coping - cognitive and behavioral efforts directed toward diminishing, ignoring, or denying stressful situations in an effort to avoid dealing with the stressor

Defense Mechanisms

  • crucial to maintain emotional homeostasis
  • continuum from adaptive to maladaptive

Adaptive Defense Mechanisms (aka, mature defenses)

  • do not distort the situation/reality
  • help accept situation, even if not comfortable
  • found in individuals with healthy, mature functioning => aware of the stressor; use stress-management technique
  • may be conscious or unconscious

Maladaptive Defense Mechanisms

  • based on avoiding, negating, or distorting reality to provide relief from the stressor
  • common among individuals whose emotions and impulsivity outperform reasoning
  • conscious or unconscious effort
  • often seen in children and may be considered normal for children based on their emotional development
  • prolong, avoid, or complicate stressors, potentially developing into symptoms of mental illness
Defense Mechanism Definition Adaptive Example Maladaptive Example
Altruism Transforming feelings and emotions by helping someone who is experiencing something similar A client, whose child died after being hit by a drunk driver, chooses to work with a local group to support others who have also lost their children to drunk drivers. N/A
Conversion An unconscious process that transforms psychological stress into physical symptoms N/A A client experiences temporary blindness after seeing their partner in an extramarital affair.
Compensation The replacement or creation of strengths in a specific skillset to offset real or perceived weaknesses in another area A client breaks their leg and is unable to play their favorite sport. While recovering, the client develops a skillset in playing chess. A nurse who is afraid of working with children decides to specialize in adult intensive care to avoid the pediatric population.
Denial Dismissing the situation as not being true or based in reality A client cannot sleep because they are worried about their son, who has been called into active military duty. The client decides to get up and watch a movie to get their mind off the situation and eventually falls asleep. A client is told that they are diagnosed with a terminal illness, believes that the health care provider mixed up their clients, and does not believe the diagnosis is correct.
Dissociation A temporary disruption in consciousness that affects the individuals perception of their environment, memory, or identity that results in a separation of self and those unpleasant thoughts or feelings into an inaccessible part of one’s psyche A student is taking an exam and feels uncomfortably cold. The student temporarily stops processing the feelings of being cold to focus on the exam. A client who was recently involved in a motor vehicle accident is unable to recall details about the event or their identity.
Displacement Redirecting unacceptable feelings or thoughts about the stressor to a less threatening target N/A A client is angry with their boss for a poor review directs their anger toward their spouse, a perceived less threatening target.
Identification Assuming the characteristics of another individual A client experiences a flat tire while driving. They think and act as their parent would in a similar situation. A child often witnesses domestic violence and begins to harm other children at school.
Intellectualization Concealing or abstractly processing emotions by means of excessive intellectual analysis or activity A nurse blocks out the emotional aspect of caring for a victim of violence to prioritize and focus on the client’s needs. A client was recently sexually assaulted seeks out information on the psychological development of perpetrators and their victims.
Projection A person's painful thoughts or emotions about themselves, something they have done, or the way they are feeling are projected to other people, places, or situations N/A A child who consistently lies to their parent assumes that the parent lies consistently as well.
Rationalization Using reasoning and logic to avoid or explain the stressor or their actions and avoid their own emotions A client feels humiliated during a presentation and states, “The audience is silent because they are interested in what I have to say.” After surviving an episode of abuse, the victim states, “This would not have happened if I just paid more attention to their (perpetrator) needs.”
Reaction Formation The replacement of unacceptable thoughts or feelings with the opposite emotion or thought A client who is trying to stop their substance use is often heard teaching others about the dangers of substance use. A client is having difficulty expressing their identity to their peers and begins to act and speak in opposite characteristics of how they actually identify.
Regression A return to a previous developmental stage of behavioral, cognitive, or emotional functioning A client comes home from work after a stressful day and decides to watch their favorite childhood television show. One person in an argument begins to yell and throw objects at the other person.
Repression A person that avoids thinking or talking about stresso, who can admit certain feelings about the stressor but cannot think about what led up to the feeling or cause of the stressor A client is providing the eulogy at their cousin’s funeral. After feelings of intense sadness, the client unconsciously decides to tell a funny story about when they were children. A client cannot remember being abused as a child but has a problem developing relationships.
Splitting The inability to accept that an individual can have both good and poor aspects but believes someone is all “bad” or all “good" N/A A client is discussing a staff member who set clear boundaries and sees this staff member as “awful and a poor excuse for a nurse.”
Sublimation Feelings and emotions that are changed when the individual chooses to take the energy behind these feelings and put them toward a constructive purpose A client is feeling emotionally exhausted after a stressful day at work and decides to go for a walk after they get home. N/A
Suppression A conscious (voluntary or purposeful) effort where the individual chooses to inhibit or control unwanted thoughts or feelings caused by the stressor A client who is angry at their parent but refuses to let it spoil a family holiday celebration. A client who is unable to work due to a recent surgery states that they will worry about paying their bills once they are fully recovered.
Undoing An attempt to remove unpleasant thoughts or emotions by engaging in the opposite behavior that caused the initial stress A client accidently breaks their siblings favorite toy and saves up money to purchase their sibling a replacement toy. A client showers their partner with affection after physically abusing them

Alleviation Techniques, Health Promotion

Adjusting expectations
Avoids disappointment, reduces stress
Consider multiple outcomes

Biofeedback

  • Biosensors monitor physiological responses
  • physical and mental exercises to help control their specific automatic physical body functions, such as heart rate, blood pressure, and temperature.
  • Clinician assists with interpretation
  • Goal: awareness + self-regulation

Deep breathing
Slow, deliberate breaths improve gas exchange
↓heart rate, ↓BP, relaxation
Refocuses stress, ↑breathing awareness
Types: figure-eight, dragon, square

Positive self-talk & reduce self-blame
Encourage kindness, not doubt or blame
Physical + mental health benefits

Guided imagery
Deep breathing + imagining calm scene
Engage senses in imagined environment
Guided verbally or via audio

Mindfulness meditation
Focus on present moment, thoughts, sensations
Accept without judgment
Recognize feelings, let them go
