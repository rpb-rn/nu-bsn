Mental Health - the foundation for emotions, thinking, communication, learning, resilience, and self-esteem. Ability to complete ADL

Mental Illness - health conditions involving changes in emotion, thinking or behavior (or a combination of these). Mental illnesses are associated with distress and/or problems functioning in social, work, or family activities."

Mental Health Disorder - a diagnostic term for a group of mental illness manifestations.

Clients Experiencing Mental Health Issues

Mental Health Continuum - range of mental health functioning with adaptive (wellness, health coping) on one end of the spectrum and maladaptive (illness, unhealthy coping, or an inability to cope) on the other end of the spectrum.

  1. Excelling - highest level of well being, in that a person has fulfillment in life and the ability for personal success. Motivations for goal achievement, rational thoughts, ability to foster social relationships, and the ability to resolve stress.
  2. Thriving - may experience stressors but can resolve those stressors. The nurse should look for plans or actions to process stress and the ability to foster social activity.
  3. Surviving - not a state of distress, but it is a state in which problems that may need action can be recognized. It may be described as the person feeling on edge. The nurse should look for nervousness, irritability, distraction, or withdrawnness.
  4. Struggling - may feel worthlessness, worried often, and that life is misery, but not all hope is lost. The nurse should look for poor performance in academic or occupational settings and dysfunctions in sleep and appetite.
  5. Crisis - severe symptoms and needs assistance. The nurse should anticipate a high level of worry, low mood, dysfunction in sleep, and irrational thinking.

Continuum of Care (Wellness-Illness Model) - views an individual’s mental health across three general areas, well-being, neutrality, and disability

  1. Well Being - healthy
  2. Neutrality - ability to learn is diminished, no manifestation of illness, able to function, complete ADL
  3. Disability - mental illness, difficulty functioning

Diathesis-Stress Model - two part theory, explains mental how mental illness develops

  1. Diathesis - individual's genetic or biological predisposition for developing an illness (family hx)
  2. Stress - change in environment (physical or emotional) => infection, job change, etc

Social Determinants of Health - direct impact on well being; psychological distress are caused by a multitude of factors, such as discrimination, prejudice, and exposure to violence experienced by individuals based on their age, gender identity, sexual orientation, race, or ethnicity.

5 Main Groups of SODH

  1. Life course - exposure to physical, environmental, and socioeconomic events at critical stages throughout one’s life
    • Most critical stage: where rapid growth and neurological development typically occur
    • Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) = Traumatic or negative events such as physical or emotional abuse and neglect, sexual abuse, and a dysfunctional homelife that increase the risk for depression, anxiety, behavior, and substance use disorders.
    • Protective Factors: social support systems, health screenings, and mental health resources,
  2. Households - Income, access to resources like food and water, parental mental and physical health, social support, and maternal care
    • Lack of insurance coverage - huge factor in decrease seeking care
  3. Community - built and natural environment, neighborhood security, and the degree to which individuals participate in community events.
  4. Local services - schools, social services, health-related services, family services, age-related services (youth and older adults), and access to clean air and water.
  5. Country-level factors - human rights, access to health care, housing needs, policies for education access, governance, and inequality.
    • Poverty = children who are poor have higher risk
    • Pollution
    • Disasters, war
    • Protective Factors: national policies, cyberbullying, etc

Bias, Stigma - Effect on Mental Health

  • Delay in seeking health - cased by stigma, bias

Stigma

  • negative attitude or discredit against an individual or an identifiable group in which the individual or group is labeled as being different.

  • can be thru media -> public stigma

  • employer can negative attitude toward mental illness

  • unemployment rate of people with M.I. - higher

  • 3 Types of Stigma

    1. Public - negative attitude toward those who have a mental illness; this can be expressed in discriminatory attitudes or actions.
    2. Self-Stigma - negative view or internalized shame regarding their condition.
    3. Institutional - governmental policies or organizations limit opportunities for those with mental illness; this can be both intentional and unintentional.

Bias

  • stigma leads to bias
  • stereotypes, prejudice, or even discrimination against a group of people that, in comparison to another group, is unfair
  • example of stereotype: depression = desire to self-harm, attention seeking

Explicit Bias - Intentionally displaying behaviors of discrimination toward someone.
Implicit Bias - bias occurs outside of one’s conscious awareness.

DSM-5

Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition, Text Revision (DSM-5-TR)

  • principal diagnostic tool for defining and diagnosing mental health conditions.
  • Conditions are continuously monitored and can be graded based on severity and absence or presence of certain features.
  • Does not include treatment!
  • tool to assist providers in using common diagnostic language to describe clients with mental health disorder

WHO International Classification of Disease (ICD) - coding system utilized by member nations of the WHO to provide a common language around the description of illnesses.

DSM5 - includes criteria for diagnosis

Legal, Ethical, Political, Shit

  • lack of health insurance => leads to decrease in seeking care for M.I.
  • clients have the right to humane treatment - consent and confidentiality, refuse treatment

JAB-FN => Just Always Be Fair & Nice
=> Justice, Autonomy, Beneficience, Fidelity, Nonmaleficence

Nursing Code of Ethics: Ethical Principals

  1. Beneficence - providers act for doing good, and preventing harm
  2. Autonomy - ability to make rational decisions related to their care.
  3. Nonmaleficence - do no harm
  4. Justice - Fair and equal care and treatment for all

Considerations:

  • balance autonomy and client safety => can present as ethical dilemmas
  • nurses may limit choices in order to keep patient safe

Involuntary Hold - admitted against their will

Right to self-determination - allows a client to refuse treatment, medz

HIPAA - Health Insurance Portability Accountability Act => need consent to release info

Duty to Warn - healthcare providers responsiblity when another patient threatens another; must report

Mistreatment - nurses are required to protect vulnerable individuals, children, elderly, disabled for maltreatment/abuse

Culturally Sensitive Nursing Care

Cultural Humility - practice of trying to appreciate and respect another individual’s culture and the role it plays in the individual’s life and health practices

Holistic Practices - acupuncture, massage therapy, natural erbs, specific diet, etc
