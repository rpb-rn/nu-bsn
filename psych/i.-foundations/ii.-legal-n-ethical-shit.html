ii. Legal n Ethical Shit

Milieu Therapy

  • A psychotherapeutic-based treatment focused on modifying or controlling the immediate environment in which the client is living.
  • Focus includes social and physical settings with the purpose of promoting health and healing.
  • more humane care to clients than asylums
    • trauma-informed - most pysch patients have traumatic past
  • reduces inappropriate behavior
  • milieau = environment
  • mental health facility should be structured in ways that provide an ongoing therapeutic environment
  • encourages self-awareness and self- management
  • basically everything that is good
  • standard of care for nurses

Components of Milieu:

  1. Physical: healing space => safe and prevention injury, low stim, privacy, home-like
    • restraints - balance with dignity; least restrictive
    • promote interactions - chairs must be conversation-like instead of line
  2. Psychosocial: creating healing culture
    • implement therapeutic social environment
    • idea: each client owns their behavior and is responsible for their choices
    • planned structure - predictability is good
    • post schedule and client rights on spaces

More components of Physical Milieu:

  • Safety & injury prevention → self/other-directed, suicide risk
  • Staff observation levels → safety, ongoing assessment
  • Environmental stimulation → noise, light, interactions
  • Adjust placement → room/unit changes based on sensitivity or need
  • Art, color, design → match clinical needs, avoid overstimulation (abstract art → confusion, anxiety, anger, depression)
  • Provide stimulation → sensory enhancement for dementia clients
  • Ensure privacy → dedicated spaces
  • Restraints/seclusion → balance dignity, independence, least restrictive goal
  • Consider cognition/physical needs → signage, wheelchair/walker accessibility

Ethics and Ethical Shit

  • basis for determining intentions, motives, and actions by a particular group or within society.
  • consideration of cultural norms, personal morals or values, and values of the profession

Mental Healthcare Act of 2017 - protects the rights of patients who have mental illnesses, provides mental health pros with ethical and legal responsibilities for patients

  • autonomy
  • nonmaleficence - do not harm
  • beneficence - removing harmful conditions to benefit the client or others.
    • example: providing meds to reduce agitation, or remove objects that could pose harm
  • fidelity - keeping promises
  • veracity - Dealing truthfully with clients and others during professional interactions.
  • justice - speak and be heard in a group session
  • confidentiality
  • informed consent
  • involuntary admission

Code Of Ethics for Nurses - outlined by the American Nurses Association

  • articulates the ethical values, duties, and standards expected of a nurse in all areas of their practice​​​​​​​.

Capacity - ability or inability to make decisions about proposed medical treatments or aspects of their health care.

Competence

  • only determined by a JUDGE thru a court hearing
  • ability to understand information, use reasonable thought processes, communicate their wishes
  • can understand the consequences of their decisions.

Laws and Legal Implications

Parity - equal coverage for mental and physical health in accessibility to health insurance.

Federal government:

  • Regulates health systems and providers.
  • Protects the rights of the individual.
  • Provides funding for services.
  • Supports research and innovation.
  • Establishes and enforces a minimum standard of mental health care.

State Government:

  • Establishes specific standards and regulations for mental health treatment.
  • Provides oversight of mental health care within the state.
  • Addresses issues related to treatment facilities, medical records, and standards, including involuntary treatment.
  • Expands on federal government programs, funding, research, and protection standards.

Types of Admission:

  1. Voluntary Admission
    • client requests evaluation or admission to a mental health facility
    • needs consent form
    • must be assessed within 72 hours => then DC or continue admission
  2. Involuntary Admission - assisted inpatient treatment.
    • severe mental illness, danger to self and others, unable to meet basic self-care
    • illness prevents them from recognizing the need to seek care
    • usually needs 2 providers to submit justification
    • Emergency Hold: must determine to DC within 72 hours (in most states) => when without court order
    • If evaluation determines patient meets criteria for commitment => court hearing - patient provided legal counsel
  3. Emergency Admission - temporary admission
    • imminent danger to themselves or others
    • usually detained by law enforcement

Client Bill of Rights - in place despite being involuntarily admitted

Exceptions to Confidentiality and Privacy:

  • danger to themselves
  • danger to others
  • leave facility against medical advice
  • suspicion of abuse (including neglect, battery, murder)

Duty To warn - notify individuals who are being threatened by a client supersedes upholding the client’s right to (Tarasoff)

Informed Consent - can apply to legal representative if client is incompetent

  1. description f treatment
  2. benefits, adverse effects
  3. risks
  4. consequence of not receiving treatment
  5. alternatives if available
  6. signed form - if applicable by state
  7. nurse to serve as witness

Implied Consent - can never be used if a patient's legal representative refuses treatment

Capacity vs Competency
Capacity

  • ability to provide informed consent
  • often assumed by provider; if needed => use cognition assessment tool
  • ability to understand information, use reasonable thought processes
  • ability to: communicate their wishes, and understand the consequences of their decisions.
    Competency
  • can only be determined in court of law during hearing
  • health care providers cannot determine this!
  • cognitive processes are intact and able to make decisions based on their personal motives
  • if incompetent, a legal surrogate, guardian is assigned

Psychiatric Advanced Directives (PAD)

  • legal tool that allows patient to outline their wishes prior to a mental health crisis
  • underutilized - need further education
  • laws vary by state

Tort

  • violation of a patient's protected rights; breach of duty of the nurse
  • intentional or unintentional

Unintentional Tort - one example is negligence (standard of care not provided)
Intentional Tort - examples include assault, battery (e.g. shaking a patient to awaken them), false imprisonment (restraints without lawful reason)

Restraints and Seclusion

  • patients should always be supervised during:
    • seclusion/restraint
    • left in a prone or supine position
  • check q15 minutes
  • Document always - include who was consulted, what was done, who was notified

False Imprisonment - confinement of a patient when there is no legal reason to do so; use of restraints/seclusion

Order of Interventions (least to most restrictive):

  1. de-escalation
  2. timeout
  3. seclusion
  4. physical/chemical restraint

De-escalation Strategies

  • Use verbal communication that is clear and calm.
  • body language of staff is nonthreatening.
  • Approach the client with respect by being supportive of their issue or concern.
  • Respond to client issues or concerns creating a sense of trust.
  • Set clear limits or boundaries.
  • low-stim environment

Seclusion vs Timeout
Timeout - to separate from a situation, allowing them time to think, calm down, prepare to end the behavior that, and reengage with others with a more composed presence and controlled behavior

Seclusion vs Restraint

  • next step from timeout
  • seclusion - last intervention before restraint

Chemical Restraints - associated with increased risk of harm, skin breakdown, falls, deterioration of mental status

  • last resort (along with physical restraints)!
  • examples: antipsychoitcs/neuroleptics, anxiolytics, mood stabilizers, sedatives

Documentations:

  • order
  • patient behavior leading up to
  • indication that least restrictive/de-escalation techniques were attempted
  • type of restraints
  • interventions while restrained
  • ongoing monitoring every 15 minutes - behavior, circulation, skin check

Debriefing - to improve quality of future responses

Team Roles/Responsibilities

Psychiatrist - diagnose medical and psychiatric illnesses, prescribe meds, give treatment orders, provide talk therapy, may specialize in children/adolescents
Psychologist - assess brain function and provide talk therapies
Social Worker/LCSW - create treatment goals, address long-term needs, provide psychotherapy
Occupational Therapist - assess skills and provide rehabilitation
Social Worker - provide social support such as housing, finances, and insurance
Counselor/Therapist - use therapeutic techniques to reduce symptoms (e.g., drama or art therapy)
Psychiatric Pharmacist - manage medications for mental health care

Client Rights

  • Right to Treatment - right to emergency treatment regardless of their ability to pay
  • Right to Refuse Treatment
    • provider can deny treatment: risks outweigh benefits
    • also includes right to second opinion
    • no surprises act - costs are told upfront

Client Bill of Rights:

  • To be treated with consideration and respectful care.
  • To make an advance directive and appoint someone to make health care decisions.
  • To obtain current and understandable information regarding their care, treatment, and potential health outcomes.
  • To refuse a recommended treatment, including medications.
  • To privacy and confidentiality, including communication and records.
  • To be informed of charges and policies.
  • To review their medical records.
  • To refuse inclusion in medical research studies.
  • To receive reasonable continuity of care.
  • To be informed of business relationships facilities or providers have that may influence care.

When on conservationaship - no right to refuse treatment
