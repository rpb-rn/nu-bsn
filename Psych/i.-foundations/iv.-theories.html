iv. Theories

Psychoanalytic Theory

  • freud
  • id, superego, ego
    • Id - instant gratification
    • Superego - concerned with morals and following rules; "ideal self", "conscience"
    • Ego - rational, realistic part of the personality that mediates between the id's impulses and the superego's moral demands

Psychodynamic Theory

  • human behavior is influenced by childhood experiences
  • behavior is based on childhood experiences; part of an individual’s conscious awareness and unconscious memories
  • experiences shape an individual’s personality.

Interpersonal Theory

  • interpersonal relationships and social experiences assist in shaping an individual’s self.
  • Three Types of Self: => based on social assessment
    • The Good Me
    • The Bad Me
    • The NOT me - unknown, or the part of an individual that is often repressed
  • anxiety is there when there is negative feedback

Peplau’s Interpersonal Relations Model

  • defines the nurse-client relationship as occurring in four distinct, overlapping phases
  • Stages:
    1. Pre-interaction - nurse reviews data, anticipates concerns
    2. Orientation - nurse explains their role; pt expresses needs, sense of belonging, etc
    3. Working - work together to achieve goals
    4. Termination - nurse and client achieved goals, end of relationship

Behavioral Theory

  • Behaviorism - focused on observed behaviors (as opposed to unconscious mental state)
  • John Watson
  • Classical Conditioning (Pavlov) - associative learning, focuses on unconscious memory
  • Operant Conditioning (B.F. Skinner) - behavior followed by positive consequences is likely to be repeated, and behavior followed by negative consequences is less likely to be repeated.

Cognitive Theory

  • human behaviors are influenced by an individual’s thought process
  • Social cognitive theory is a form of cognitive therapy in which an individual models behaviors observed by others (e.g. peer pressure)

Humanistic Theory

  • Humanism - individual is studied or viewed as a whole
  • Maslow, Rogers, Bugental
  • "people are innately good"
  • Mental or social illness causes deviations from this core concept
  • Rogers - individuals perceive the world based on their own experiences, which are then reflected in their personality and behaviors to meet self-actualization.
  • Bugental - humans are a single entity and cannot be broken down into parts

Developmental Theory

  • begin with child development and progress throughout the lifespan with distinct stages describing behavioral differences
  • Piaget - human intelligence is based on one’s biological ability to adapt to one’s environment; intelligence advances with age
  • Erikson - each stage has specific tasks or goals to be plotted out and achieved so that resolution can occur
  • see Developmental Theories

Moral Developmental Theory

  • based on Lawrence Kohlberg’s stages

  • not tied to a specific age group, but rather to levels that can often be noted at specific ages

    *Preconventional Level (self-interest, obedience, avoid punishment)*

    • Stage 1: Obedience and Punishment – morality based on avoiding punishment, rules seen as fixed
    • Stage 2: Instrumental Relativist Orientation – right action based on self-interest, fair exchange or “you scratch my back, I’ll scratch yours”

    *Conventional Level (conformity, social order)*

    • Stage 3: Interpersonal Relationships – morality defined by approval, “good boy/good girl” orientation, pleasing others
    • Stage 4: Law and Order – respect for authority, duty, following laws to maintain society

    *Postconventional Level (abstract reasoning, universal principles)*

    • Stage 5: Social Contract and Individual Rights – laws as social contracts, can be changed for the greater good, recognition of differing values
    • Stage 6: Universal Ethical Principles – "golden rule"; morality based on internal principles of justice and equality, may conflict with laws

Biophysical Model

  • provides an explanation of mental health disorders as a malfunction within the body
  • offers insight into how the brain’s anatomy, genomics, psychopharmacology, and neurotransmitters influence mental health
