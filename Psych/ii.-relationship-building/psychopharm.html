Neurobiological Causes of Mental Illness

Genetics

  • mental illnesss can be inheritable
  • Neurobiology - functioning of bran impacts behavior
  • example: twins with separate living environment can develop mental illness, addiction is passed down from parent to child

Psychiatric Pharmacogenomic Testing

  • study that seeks to understand how the genetics of an individual may impact the interaction on psychotropic mediccations
  • can show severity of side effects

Psychoimmunology

  • studies impact of psychosocial stress on the body’s immune system
  • relationship between stress and the immune system may be related to the presentation of some manifestations of mental illness

Infection => viral infection possibly alters genetic composition in the central nervous system leading to the presentation of mental illness

Neuroplasticity

  • brain’s ability to adapt synaptic connections, particularly in response to stress or learning or after experiencing an injury
  • ability to establish new neuronal pathways or go through complete neuronal remapping in response to a stimulus
  • may take weeks or months to reconfigure pathways
  • Explains why: some manifestations of mental illness increase in severity over time, such as Major Depressive Disorder, and cannot be cured through a single dose of medication
  • Explains why: pysch meds take weeks to take effect

Serious Side Effects

Serotonin Syndrome

  • too much serotonin
  • causes: too much SSRI, migrain/pain meds, LSD, ecstasy, combining antidepressants with St. John's wort
  • Manifestations: restlessness, high BP, tachycardia, dilated pupils, muscle rigidity, sweating, loss of muscle coordinator
  • Treatment: sedate with benzodiazepines, serotonin antagonist medications like cyproheptadine.

Activation Syndrome

  • can potentially happen when starting SSRI
  • manifestations of irritability, anxiety, impulsivity, aggressiveness, and agitation
  • High risk for suicidal ideations
  • may develop within the first few hours of administration through the first few weeks

Antidepressant Discontinuation Syndrome

  • sudden discontinuation of the antidepressant medication
  • difficulty sleeping, anxiety, depression, and flu-like symptoms
  • rare: electric-shock-like sensations felt in their heads that can disrupt ADLs
  • stopping MAOIs - psychosis
  • may persist for weeks

Lithium Toxicity

  • anything greater than 1.5 mEq/L
  • Causes: dehydration, overdose, concurrent use with Loop Diuretics
  • Manifestations:
    • Early Toxicity
      • 1.5 mEq/L poor coordination, confusion, and sedation
      • GI discomfort, nausea, coarse tremors, vomiting, and diarrhea
      • Treatment: withhold, consult provider
    • Advanced => 2.0-2.5
      • seizures and stupor
      • Extremely diluted urine, blurred vision, respiratory complications, tinnitus, and jerking motor movements
      • Treatment: urea or mannitol to increase kidney excretion, gastric lavage
    • Severe => greater than 2.5 mEq/L
      • rapid deterioration -> comatose -> death
      • treat: dialysis

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)

  • initial exposure or abrupt continuation of Antipsychotics
  • FGA => higher risk
  • muscle rigidity, hyperthermia, vital sign instability (increased/decreased blood pressure and tachycardia), and elevated creatine kinase
  • Nursing Intervention: monitoring fluid and electrolytes, initiating cooling measures, and placing the client on cardiac monitoring
  • Treatment: dopaminergic agent, such as bromocriptine, or a skeletal muscle relaxing medication such as dantrolene

Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS)

  • effect of blocking dopamine transmission from the midbrain to the brainstem.
  • Types:
    • Akathisia: Restlessness, pacing, fidgeting
    • Dystonia: Muscle stiffness, spasms, locked gaze
    • Parkinsonism: Tremors, rigidity, slow movements, slumped posture, shuffling gait, drooling, tremors, and pill-rolling movements of the fingers
    • Tardive dyskinesia: Involuntary movements of the face, tongue, and limbs
  • Treatment: anticholinergic => diphenhydramine, benzotropine; or valbenazine
    • Usually: B52 - benadryl, haldol, ativan
  • Abnormal Involuntary Movement Scale (AIMS) is an evidence-based assessment of the level of tardive dyskinesia based on abnormal movement.

Agranulocytosis

  • caused by Antipsychotics => usually Clozaril / Clozipine (Atypical, or Second Gen Antipsychotics)
  • Manifestations: flu-like, fatigue, sore throat, fatigue, fever, muscle aches
  • very dangerous

Detoxification Protocol

Alcohol Withdrawal

  • prolonged CNS depressant => decreased GABA, increased, Gluatamate
  • heart rate, increased blood pressure, nausea, vomiting, increased seizure risk, and hallucinations
  • Treatment: benzodiazepine

Opiate Withdrawal

  • desensitized opiate receptor signaling
  • large amounts of norepinephrine are released causing manifestations like muscle aches, goose flesh, GI discomfort, and sweating
  • Life-threatening: nausea, vomitting, diarrhea -> dehydration, e- imbalance
  • treat: methadone or buprenorphine (Suboxone => added naloxone)

Detox Protocol

  • Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol-Revised (CIWA-Ar)
  • Clinical Opiate Withdrawal Scale (COWS)

Steps:

  1. use appropriate scale - CIWA/COWS
  2. rate severity of withdrawal manifestations
  3. calculate sum of categories
  4. calculate med dose per orders
  5. administer meds
