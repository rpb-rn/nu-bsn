Insulin

Types of Insulin

note: give food when insulin peaks (glucose less than 70)

Rapid-acting insulin (e.g., Lispro/Humalog, Aspart/NovoLog)

  • Onset: 15-30 minutes
  • Peak: 1–2 hours
  • Duration: 3–5 hours
  • Given before meals to control postprandial blood sugar spikes
  • ASSPART = move you ass, this is fast

REGULAR or SHORT-acting insulin (e.g., Regular insulin, Humulin R, Novolin R)

  • Onset: 30–60 minutes
  • Peak: 2–4 hours
  • Duration: 4-12 hours
  • Often used in hospital settings for acute control.

Intermediate-acting insulin (e.g., NPH - aka CLOUDY insulin) => N = not fast

  • Onset: 1–2 hours
  • Peak: 4–12 hours
  • Duration: 14-24 hours
  • slower release, kind of like regular gas, but with a longer burn time => basal control
  • usually taken twice a day
  • never IV

Long-acting insulin (e.g., Glargine/Lantus, Detemir)

  • Onset: 1–2 hours
  • Peak: No pronounced peak
  • Duration: Up to 24 hours
  • Provides basal insulin coverage, typically administered once daily.

Premixed insulin

  • A combination of short or rapid-acting and intermediate-acting insulins.
  • Given for those who require both rapid and basal control with fewer injections.

How to Mix

  • commonly mixed: NPH (intermediate) and Regular (short acting)
  • ROLL THE VIAL for CLOUDY - do not shake
  • clean with alcohol swab
  • Lantus cannot be mixed with anything

NR-RN: I am NOT RETIRED, I am an R-N

  • insert AIR ONLY in order : NPH first, then Regular (not-retired)
  • once air is inside on both vials, proceed to draw from pressurized vials
  • Draw in order R-N: Regular first, NPH last

Alternative way of remembering: Cloudy to clear, clear to cloudy (air then draw)

Administration

  • Subcutaneous injection (most common) - rotate sites regularly (abdomen, upper arms, thighs, or buttocks)
  • Insulin pump (continuous infusion)
  • Rotation of injection sites (avoid lipodystrophy)
  • Check blood sugar levels before administration (to adjust dose accordingly).
  • Vials vs. pens (insulin pens are easier for patients and caregivers to use).
  • Correct storage (refrigerate unused insulin, do not freeze).

Monitoring

  • Blood glucose monitoring before meals and at bedtime (typically 4 times a day, but can vary).
  • A1C levels (target: <7% for most patients).
  • Signs of hypoglycemia (shakiness, sweating, confusion, dizziness, headache).
  • Signs of hyperglycemia (increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurred vision).
  • Monitor for signs of insulin resistance (requiring dose adjustment).

Hypoglycemia Management

  • Mild hypoglycemia (BG <70 mg/dL): Consume 15-20g of quick-acting carbohydrate (e.g., 4 oz fruit juice, 3–4 glucose tablets).
  • Severe hypoglycemia: Administer glucagon (IM) or 25–50mL of D50 (IV) if unconscious or unable to ingest oral glucose.
  • Recheck BG after 15 minutes and repeat treatment if needed.

Patient Education

  • Signs of hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia and how to respond to each.
  • Insulin self-administration technique (rotation of injection sites, proper needle disposal).
  • Dietary management (counting carbs, meal timing).
  • Exercise considerations (adjusting insulin doses based on activity levels).
  • Stress management (stress can raise blood sugar).
  • Glucagon use (for patients at risk of severe hypoglycemia).
  • Importance of regular follow-up (A1C checks, eye exams, kidney function).

Nursing Considerations

  • Adjust insulin doses based on current blood glucose levels, meal plans, and physical activity.
  • Ensure proper patient education on the correct insulin use, lifestyle changes, and recognizing symptoms of hypo/hyperglycemia.
  • Monitor for complications such as infection at injection sites or delayed wound healing.
  • Assist with managing insulin pumps (ensure proper site placement, troubleshoot equipment).
  • Coordinate care with dietitians and endocrinologists for individualized care plans.
