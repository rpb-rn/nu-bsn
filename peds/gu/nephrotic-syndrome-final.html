Nephrotic Syndrome - final

  • Kidney disorder causing massive protein loss in urine
  • Characterized by proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, edema, and hyperlipidemia
  • Nephrotic Syndrome => Nasty Protein Loss
  • Most common in children ages 2–7, especially males

Pathophysiology

  • Damage to glomerular basement membrane increases permeability to proteins
  • Loss of albumin leads to decreased oncotic pressure → edema
  • Liver compensates by increasing lipoprotein synthesis → hyperlipidemia
  • Loss of immunoglobulins and clotting factors increases infection and clot risk

Causes

  • 4 S => Triggers:
    1. stress
    2. sickness
    3. smoking
    4. sun exposure
  • Minimal change disease (most common in children) => idiopathic
  • Focal segmental glomerulosclerosis
  • Membranous nephropathy
  • Secondary to infections, lupus, diabetes, NSAIDs

Risk Factors

  • Age 2–7 years
  • Recent viral illness
  • Allergies or immunizations
  • Autoimmune conditions
  • Genetic predisposition

Manifestations

  • Massive edema (periorbital, progressing to whole body) => Nursing tip: important to provide thorough skin care to prevent infection (ATI)
  • Weight gain with low urine output
  • Frothy or foamy urine (proteinuria)
  • Hypoalbuminemia
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Normal or slightly elevated BP
  • Fatigue, anorexia, pallor

Complications

  • Infection (peritonitis, sepsis)
  • Thromboembolism (DVT, PE)
  • Hypovolemia
  • AKI
  • Relapse or steroid resistance

Diagnostic

  • Urinalysis: 3+ or 4+ protein, microscopic hematuria
  • Low serum albumin, low total protein
  • Elevated cholesterol and triglycerides
  • Renal biopsy if atypical presentation
  • Normal complement levels (differentiates from APSGN)

Treatment

  • Corticosteroids (prednisone is first-line)
  • Diuretics for severe edema
  • Albumin IV followed by furosemide in severe hypoalbuminemia
  • Statins if hyperlipidemia is prolonged
  • Immunosuppressants for steroid-resistant cases
  • Antibiotics if infection present

Nursing Considerations

  • Daily weights and strict I&Os
  • Monitor edema and urine output
  • Prevent skin breakdown
  • Monitor for signs of infection or thromboembolism
  • Promote quiet activities during steroid therapy
  • Administer meds and monitor for side effects (e.g., Cushingoid from steroids)

Patient Education

  • Importance of med adherence, especially steroids

  • Recognize relapse signs: weight gain, edema, decreased urine

  • Avoid exposure to infections; may be immunocompromise

  • Low-sodium, adequate protein diet

  • Monitor weight and urine at home

  • Report signs of blood clots or infection

  • NAILS = Nephrosis, Albumin low, Increased lipids, Lipiduria, Swelling

  • HEPOn = Hypoalbuminemia, Edema, Proteinuria, hyperlipidemia
