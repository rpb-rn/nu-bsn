Patent Ductus Arteriosus

  • persistent opening between aorta and pulmonary artery after birth
  • normal in fetal circulation; should close within hours to days postnatally
  • failure to close leads to left-to-right shunt

Pathophysiology

  • aortic pressure > pulmonary artery pressure → oxygenated blood flows into lungs
  • ↑ pulmonary blood flow → pulmonary congestion, ↑ workload on LV
  • large PDA can lead to CHF, pulmonary hypertension
  • chronic flow can reverse (Eisenmenger) → cyanosis in lower extremities

Clinical Presentation

  • may be asymptomatic if small
  • large PDA: tachypnea, poor feeding, failure to thrive, frequent infections
  • classic murmur: continuous "machinery-like" murmur at left upper sternal border
  • widened pulse pressure, bounding pulses, active precordium

Diagnostic Tools

  • echocardiogram confirms diagnosis and shunt direction
  • chest x-ray: cardiomegaly, pulmonary vascular markings
  • ECG: LV hypertrophy or biventricular hypertrophy if large PDA
  • oxygen challenge test may help differentiate from other CHDs

Causes

  • failure of ductus to close, especially in premature infants
  • associated with congenital rubella syndrome

Risk Factors

  • prematurity (most common), high altitude birth, female sex*
  • maternal infections (e.g., rubella), congenital anomalies

Treatment

  • indomethacin or ibuprofen to promote closure in preemies
  • surgical ligation or catheter-based coil embolization if meds fail or in full-term infants
  • monitor small PDAs, may close spontaneously

Nursing Interventions

  • monitor respiratory effort, signs of CHF
  • administer and monitor effects of NSAID therapy
  • ensure adequate nutrition (high-calorie feeds, NG if needed)
  • post-op care if closed surgically: monitor vitals, perfusion, infection signs

Systemic Effects

  • chronic volume overload of LA and LV → CHF
  • risk of endocarditis
  • if Eisenmenger develops: differential cyanosis (cyanotic toes, pink fingers)

Complications

  • pulmonary hypertension, CHF, failure to thrive*
  • infective endocarditis, Eisenmenger syndrome if untreated
  • possible renal hypoperfusion in severe cases
