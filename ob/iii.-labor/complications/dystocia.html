Dystocia

  • Abnormal or difficult labor resulting from problems with components of labor: 4 P's
    • powers - expulsive forces
    • passageway
    • passenger or fetal development
    • psyche or maternal stress
  • It is a leading cause of cesarean delivery

Umbrella Term for "failure to progress" with labor due to:

  • lac of progressive cervical dilation
  • lack of descent of fetal head
  • or both

Causes

POWERS

  1. Ineffective uterine contractions (hypotonic or hypertonic)
    • Hypertonic Uterine Dysfunction - erratic contractions; irregular, uncoordinated, ineffective => uterus never fully relaxes between contractions
      treatment: sedate, analgesia, hydrate, reposition
    • Hypotonic Uterine Dysfunction - Contractions become poor in quality and lack sufficient intensity to dilate and efface the cervix; high risk for hemorrhage
      treatment: augment via Pitocin, amniptomy to enchance labor, hydrate
  2. Protracted disorders - very slow dilation and descent
  3. Arrest Disorders - no progress or failure of descent
  4. Precipitate Labor - completed in 3 hours or less; "cannonball baby"
    => too fast of delivery => cervical lacerations, uterine rupture

PASSENGER

  1. Positioning => any position other than occiput anterior (head down and anterior facing) = higher risk
    • Persistent Occiput Posterior - most common
    • Shoulder Distocia - head delivered but shoulder is blocking; EMERGENCY
      => can lead to brachial plexus palsy - no nerve control, shoulder is drooping
      => high risk for uterine lacerations, bleeding
      => "turtling" - head of the baby emerging from the birth canal, then retracting back
  2. Multiple Gestation - twins or more (plan for vaginal birth if twins are VERTEX)
  3. Macrosomia - 4kg or more baby

PASSAGEWAY

  1. Pelvis Shape = "Good Anatomy Allows Passage"
    • Gynecoid - good; best shape, round
    • Anthropoid - oval, "ape-like", okay for delivery
    • Android - "andro" => male pevis, difficult!
    • Platypelloid - platypus=flatl POOR FIT!
  2. obstructions of maternal birth canal - swelling of cervix

FetoPelvic Disproportion (FPD) - Fetal head is too large or pelvis is too small

PSYCHE

  • mom emotions => psychological stress
  • hormones from intense anxiety
  • anxiety => increased fer, tension, pain tolerance

Risk Factors

  • First-time pregnancy (nulliparity)
  • Advanced maternal age
  • Obesity
  • Epidural anesthesia
  • Prolonged labor
  • Hydramnios
  • Short maternal status (less than 5 ft tall)
  • More than 8.8 lbs fetus
  • GA >41 wks
  • Abnormal fetal presentation (e.g., occiput posterior, breech)
  • Chorioamnionitis
  • high caffeine intake
  • Ineffective uterine contractions
  • High fetal station at complete cervical dilation

note: acrocyanosis is normal

Clinical Manifestations

  • Slowed or stalled cervical dilation
  • Lack of fetal descent despite adequate contractions
  • Abnormal contraction patterns
  • Maternal fatigue
  • Fetal distress may develop

Complications

  • Prolonged labor
  • Fetal hypoxia or distress
  • Uterine rupture (especially with prior C-section)
  • Maternal infection or hemorrhage
  • Increased likelihood of operative delivery
  • Erb's Palsy

Diagnostic

  • Monitor Progress of Labor - 1cm per hour dilation is standard
  • Physical exam to assess dilation, effacement, and fetal station
  • Ultrasound for fetal position and estimated weight
  • Intrauterine pressure catheter (IUPC) to measure contraction strength
  • Pelvimetry in suspected CPD

Treatment

  • Amniotomy to augment labor => breaking water
  • Oxytocin (Pitocin) for hypotonic contractions
  • Upright positioning - promote fetal rotation and descent; change frequently
  • Operative delivery (vacuum, forceps) if indicated
  • Cesarean delivery if labor fails to progress or complications arise
  • suprapubic bone for shoulder dystocia

Nursing Considerations

  • Monitor labor progression closely
  • Assess fetal heart rate and uterine contraction pattern
  • Encourage position changes and ambulation if appropriate
  • Administer pain management and oxytocin as ordered
  • backrubs to promote fetal rotation

Patient Education

  • Explain interventions and rationale (e.g., amniotomy, oxytocin)
  • Discuss signs of labor progress and when to alert staff
  • Provide reassurance and emotional support
  • Prepare patient for possible surgical delivery if needed
