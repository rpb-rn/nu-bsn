Methergine (Methylergonovine)

  • Indications: Prevention and treatment of postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) caused by uterine atony or subinvolution.
  • Mechanism of Action: Directly stimulates smooth muscle of the uterus, causing rapid, sustained tetanic uterine contraction; also constricts peripheral and coronary blood vessels.
  • Contraindications: Hypersensitivity to methergine, hypertension (pre-eclampsia, eclampsia, chronic hypertension), cardiac disease, peripheral vascular disease, hepatic or renal impairment, sepsis, threatened spontaneous abortion (before delivery of placenta), induction of labor.
  • Expected Side Effects: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain/cramping, dizziness, headache, diaphoresis.
  • Adverse Effects: Severe hypertension (potentially leading to cerebral hemorrhage), seizures, palpitations, chest pain, myocardial infarction, dyspnea, pulmonary edema, stroke, allergic reactions (rash, anaphylaxis), ergotism (rare, with prolonged use or overdose: peripheral vasospasm, cyanosis, cold extremities).
  • Nursing Considerations: Assess blood pressure frequently (every 15 minutes or more often as indicated) during administration and for several hours afterward; monitor uterine tone, vaginal bleeding, and fundal height; administer IV slowly over at least 60 seconds (rapid IV push can cause severe hypertension and cerebrovascular accident); monitor for signs of ergotism; educate patient about potential side effects and to report any severe headache, chest pain, or vision changes; typically given IM; IV is reserved for emergent situations due to risk of severe hypertension.
  • Specific Parameters to Discontinue Med: 140/90 BP or greater; signs of severe hypertension or neurological symptoms (e.g., severe headache, visual disturbances, seizures); chest pain or signs of myocardial ischemia.

Carboprost Tromethamine (Hemabate)

  • Indications: Treatment of postpartum hemorrhage due to uterine atony that is unresponsive to other oxytocic agents; used in pregnancy termination.
  • Mechanism of Action: Potent prostaglandin F2-alpha analog; directly stimulates sustained contraction of uterine smooth muscle; also causes contraction of bronchial and vascular smooth muscle.
  • Contraindications: Hypersensitivity to carboprost or prostaglandins, acute pelvic inflammatory disease, active cardiac, pulmonary, renal, or hepatic disease, asthma (absolute contraindication due to bronchoconstriction); caution with hypertension, hypotension, anemia, jaundice, diabetes, epilepsy, previous uterine surgery (e.g., C-section).
  • Expected Side Effects: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (very common due to smooth muscle stimulation), fever, chills, flushing, headache, tachycardia.
  • Adverse Effects: Bronchospasm, wheezing, dyspnea, hypertension or hypotension, pulmonary edema, Uterine Rupture (rare, especially with overuse or in grand multiparas), uterine hypertonus, tetanic contractions, arrhythmias, DIC (disseminated intravascular coagulation, rare).
  • Nursing Considerations: Administer deep IM only; avoid IV administration; rotate injection sites if multiple doses are needed; monitor uterine tone, vaginal bleeding, and vital signs (BP, pulse, respirations, temperature) frequently; have antiemetics and antidiarrheals readily available and consider pre-medication; auscultate lung sounds for signs of bronchospasm; assess for fever, which is a common but usually transient side effect; educate patient about common GI side effects.
  • Specific Parameters to Discontinue Med: Severe bronchospasm or respiratory distress; sustained severe hypertension or hypotension refractory to treatment; signs of uterine rupture.

Tranexamic Acid (TXA)

  • Indications: Treatment of postpartum hemorrhage when conservative management is insufficient; reduces blood loss in various surgical procedures and trauma.
  • Mechanism of Action: Antifibrinolytic agent; reversibly binds to plasminogen and plasmin, preventing fibrinolysis (breakdown of blood clots); stabilizes existing clots.
  • Contraindications: Hypersensitivity to TXA, active intravascular clotting (e.g., DIC, unless primary fibrinolysis is predominant), history of thrombosis (DVT, PE, stroke, MI), unless benefits clearly outweigh risks, severe renal impairment (dose adjustment needed), history of seizures (may increase seizure risk), color vision impairment (can mask retinal toxicity).
  • Expected Side Effects: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, dizziness, headache, hypotension (if administered too rapidly IV), abdominal pain.
  • Adverse Effects: Thromboembolic events (DVT, PE, stroke, MI) – although studies suggest low risk in PPH; seizures (especially with rapid IV administration or high doses); visual disturbances, impaired color vision; allergic reactions; renal cortical necrosis (rare, with prolonged use/high doses).
  • Nursing Considerations: Administer intravenously slowly over 10 minutes to prevent hypotension; monitor for signs of thrombosis (e.g., leg pain, swelling, chest pain, shortness of breath, neurological changes); assess for any changes in vision or color perception; monitor vital signs, uterine tone, and blood loss; ensure proper hydration; typically given within 3 hours of birth for PPH benefit.
  • Specific Parameters to Discontinue Med: Signs of active thrombosis (e.g., new onset chest pain, dyspnea, calf pain, neurological deficits); severe allergic reaction; new onset or worsening seizures; significant visual changes.

Oxytocin (Pitocin)

  • Indications: Prevention and treatment of postpartum hemorrhage due to uterine atony; induction and augmentation of labor; to promote milk let-down (nasal spray, rare).
  • Mechanism of Action: Synthetic polypeptide hormone structurally identical to natural oxytocin; acts on oxytocin receptors in the uterus, causing rhythmic contractions of uterine smooth muscle; increases the frequency and intensity of contractions; also has antidiuretic effects at high doses.
  • Contraindications: Hypersensitivity to oxytocin, cephalopelvic disproportion (CPD), unfavorable fetal position or presentation, significant placenta previa or vasa previa, umbilical cord prolapse, uterine hypertonicity or fetal distress when delivery is not imminent, severe toxemia, active genital herpes, undelivered placenta (before delivery of placenta in PPH management, it's typically given after delivery).
  • Expected Side Effects: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain/cramping, headache, flushing.
  • Adverse Effects: Maternal: Uterine hyperstimulation/tetany (leading to uterine rupture, placental abruption, fetal distress), water intoxication (hyponatremia, cerebral edema, seizures, coma) with high doses and large volumes of hypotonic fluids, hypotension or hypertension (less common), arrhythmias, postpartum hemorrhage (paradoxical, if receptors are saturated or overstimulated), allergic reactions; Fetal: Fetal distress (due to uterine hyperstimulation), bradycardia, hypoxia, acidosis.
  • Nursing Considerations: Administer as a dilute IV infusion via a pump, typically as a piggyback into a main IV line; never administer undiluted IV push in PPH management (can cause severe hypotension or cardiac arrhythmias); monitor uterine activity (frequency, duration, intensity of contractions), uterine resting tone, and vaginal bleeding; monitor vital signs (BP, pulse) frequently; assess intake and output to monitor for water intoxication; assess for signs of uterine hyperstimulation (contractions lasting >90 seconds, less than 2 minutes apart, or resting tone >20 mmHg); have tocolytics (e.g., terbutaline) available to relax the uterus in case of hyperstimulation.
  • Specific Parameters to Discontinue Med: Signs of uterine hyperstimulation (contractions too frequent, too long, or resting tone too high); signs of fetal distress (if used antepartum); signs of water intoxication (e.g., hyponatremia, decreased urine output, confusion, seizures); significant maternal hypotension or arrhythmia.

Misoprostol (Cytotec)

  • Indications: Treatment of postpartum hemorrhage due to uterine atony; cervical ripening and labor induction; abortion (medical); prevention of NSAID-induced gastric ulcers.
  • Mechanism of Action: Synthetic prostaglandin E1 analog; causes strong uterine contractions; also causes cervical softening and dilation.
  • Contraindications: Hypersensitivity to misoprostol or other prostaglandins, known allergy to other prostaglandins; in the context of PPH, generally few absolute contraindications once the baby and placenta are delivered, as it's a critical situation; however, caution in patients with glaucoma, asthma (less of a concern than carboprost but still a prostaglandin), or severe cardiovascular disease; active gastrointestinal disease (if given orally for ulcer prevention).
  • Expected Side Effects: Shivering, chills, fever (very common), nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (common), abdominal pain/cramping, headache.
  • Adverse Effects: Uterine hyperstimulation/tetany (leading to uterine rupture, especially with high doses or in cases of previous uterine surgery), hypotension or hypertension, bradycardia or tachycardia, allergic reactions, bronchospasm (less common than with carboprost), DIC (rare).
  • Nursing Considerations: Can be administered orally, sublingually, rectally, or vaginally for PPH; rectal administration is often preferred in emergent PPH due to rapid absorption and less GI side effects; monitor uterine tone, vaginal bleeding, and vital signs (BP, pulse, temperature) frequently; anticipate and manage shivering/chills with warm blankets; provide comfort measures for nausea and vomiting; educate patient about common side effects; ensure accurate dosage and route of administration.
  • Specific Parameters to Discontinue Med: Sustained uterine hypertonus not responding to other measures; signs of uterine rupture; severe allergic reaction.
