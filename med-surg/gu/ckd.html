#nursing #medsurg #genitourinary

Overview of Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

  • gradual loss of kidney function over time
  • will lead to ESRD

Differences between AKI:

  • AKI is usually reversible, CKD is not
  • CKD is progressive, AKI is sudden (hence the term "acute")
Stage GFR (mL/min/1.73m²) Description Symptoms Treatment
1 ≥ 90 Normal kidney function None, possible damage Control underlying conditions
2 60-89 Mild decrease Mild symptoms Monitor kidney function
3a 45-59 Mild-moderate decrease Fatigue, mild edema Tight control of BP, glucose
3b 30-44 Moderate-severe decrease Increased fatigue, edema Refer to nephrologist, monitor
4 15-29 Severe decrease Symptoms of kidney failure Prepare for dialysis/transplant
5 < 15 Kidney failure (ESRD) Uremic symptoms Dialysis or transplant

Just note that normal GFR is ≥ 90

Causes

  • Diabetes T2 => most common cause (Diabetes T1 can also be cause but less rare)
  • Hypertension
  • Glomerulonephritis
  • Polycystic Kidney Disease
  • Obstructions like Renal Calculi, BPH, tumors
  • Chronic use of NSAIDs

Risk Factors

  • Metabolic Syndrome
  • smoking
  • higher risk: Asian, Hispanic, and Black
  • male 65 and older

Manifestations

  • Early stage (mild to moderate): no symptoms in earlier stages; can be vague symptoms like fatigue, edema, changes in urination
  • Fluid overload: edema, jugular vein distention, crackles in lungs, uncontrolled hypertension
  • anemia (causes fatigue) => because of low EPO (produced by the kidneys), EPO produces RBC
  • e- imbalance
  • metabolic acidosis
  • Neuro: lethargy, confusion, hand tremors, sleep disturbances, unusual behavior, and decreased mental sharpness
  • pruritus (itchy skin)
  • urine changes: dark, foamy, bloody urine
  • metallic taste in mouth
  • poor appetite & unintentional weight loss
  • Uremic Pericarditis: S3 gallop or pericardial friction rub may be present
  • uremic frost crystals - crystals form on the skin because your kidneys are so shot that waste builds up in your blood, causing it to sweat out and leave nasty, gritty white crystals all over you

Complications

  • ESRD
  • Anemia: decreased erythropoietin production leads to lower red blood cell count
  • Cardiovascular Disease: Increased risk of heart attack, stroke, or heart failure due to CKD's effect on blood vessels constant fluid overload
  • Metabolic Acidosis
  • Electrolyte Imbalances: Such as hyperkalemia (high potassium) or hyperphosphatemia
  • Bone Mineral Disorder: Alterations in calcium and phosphate balance, leading to weakened bones.

Diagnostic

  • GFR
  • CMP: BUN, Creatinine, e-
  • UA: + for protein or blood
  • Urine Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio: A marker for kidney damage.
  • Ultrasound or CT Scan: To evaluate kidney size, cysts, or obstructions.
  • Kidney Biopsy: In some cases, to determine the exact cause of CKD.

Treatment

  • fluid restrictions
  • Diuretics
  • Manage BP: Hypertension meds: ACEi, ARBS
  • Diet: low protein (to reduce kidney workload = avoid uremia), Na + K restrictions
  • Manage anemia: ESA and iron supplements
  • Dialysis: hemo or peritoneal for ESRD
  • Kidney transplant for ESRD

Nursing Considerations

  • strict I/O
  • monitor daily weight
  • control blood glucose
  • avoid nephrotoxins: NSAID, smoking, alcohol

Diet

  • limit: phosphate, potassium, sodium => hard to excrete
  • protein restriction

Dialysis Considerations

  1. Monitor vital signs (BP, HR, temp) before, during, and after dialysis.
  2. Weigh the patient before and after dialysis to assess fluid removal.
  3. Care for the dialysis access site, checking for infection, clotting, or poor flow.
  4. Monitor for electrolyte imbalances and adjust dialysis as needed.
  5. Hold medications like antihypertensives before dialysis.
  6. Educate the patient on treatment, diet, fluid restrictions, and adherence.
  7. Watch for complications like dialysis disequilibrium syndrome, air embolism, or bleeding.
  8. Provide emotional and psychosocial support to manage chronic illness.
  9. Ensure proper anticoagulation and monitor post-dialysis for side effects (hypotension, cramps).
  10. Practice infection control and aseptic technique when accessing the dialysis port.

Top Nursing Diagnoses

  1. Excess Fluid Volume - Related to decreased kidney filtration, fluid retention.
  2. Risk for Electrolyte Imbalance - Related to impaired renal function, decreased excretion of electrolytes.
  3. Imbalanced Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements - Related to decreased appetite, nausea, and vomiting.
  4. Fatigue - Related to anemia, uremia, altered metabolism, decreased kidney function.
  5. Risk for Infection - Related to impaired immune system, dialysis access sites.
  6. Risk for Impaired Skin Integrity - Related to edema, frequent dialysis, uremic pruritus.
  7. Disturbed Body Image - Related to physical changes (e.g., edema, dialysis access devices).
  8. Activity Intolerance - Related to decreased oxygenation, fatigue, and weakness.
  9. Risk for Impaired Gas Exchange - Related to pulmonary edema, fluid overload, respiratory distress.
  10. Chronic Pain - Related to renal disease, uremic pruritus, musculoskeletal pain.
