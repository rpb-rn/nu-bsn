Headaches

Overview

  • pain from stretching, constriction, dilation, or nociceptor stimulation of muscle fibers, meninges, facial structures, blood vessels, or cranial or spinal nerves
  • varies in location, intensity (sharp, throbbing, or a dull ache)

Primary Headaches - not caused by other conditions; examples: migraines, cluster headaches, tension-type headaches (TTH)
Secondary - caused by other conditions such as trauma, infections, ICP, vascular disorders, etc

Migraines - recurrent headaches that last for four to 72 hr; from change in cerebral perfusion; not fully understood

Status Migrainosis - lasts for more than 72 hours

Migraine Triggers and Risk Factors

  • Head trauma
  • Caffeine
  • Foods containing nitrates or tyramine
  • Artificial sweeteners
  • Hormonal changes
  • Bright lights
  • Strong odors
  • Changes in weather
  • Stress
  • Skipping meals

Causes & Risk Factors of SECONDARY Headaches

  • Carbon monoxide poisoning
  • Cerebrovascular Accident
  • Intracranial hemorrhage (see Subarachnoid Hemorrhage - blood in space under the arachnoid membrane, most commonly from trauma/rupture of an aneurysm)
  • Hypertension emergencies
  • Meningitis
  • Encephalitis
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma
  • Brain tumor

Manifestations

Tension Type Headaches (TTH)

  • mild/moderate pain on both sides of head
  • dull/band-like pressure
  • Muscle tightness in the shoulder, neck, or back
  • associated with sleep disturbances
  • lasts 30 min to 7 days

Cluster Headaches

  • Severe unilateral head pain, often behind the eye
  • Lacrimation, nasal congestion, eyelid/facial swelling, ptosis may occur (ptosis - drooping eyelid)
  • Excruciating, nonfluctuating pain; clients may pace or rock
  • Duration: 15 min to hours, may occur multiple times daily

Migraine Headaches - 4 Phases

Prodormal Phase (Hours to Days Before Headache)

  • Sensitivity to light, sound, odor
  • Fatigue, yawning
  • Food cravings, excessive thirst
  • Mood swings
  • Constipation or diarrhea

Aura Phase (Lasts ~60 min, Precedes Headache, Reversible Symptoms, Affects ~25%)

  • Visual, sensory, motor disturbances
  • Tunnel vision, blindness, visual field loss
  • Heaviness in limbs
  • Speech/language disturbances
  • Scintillating scotoma
  • Tingling in face/limbs

Headache Phase (Unilateral, Throbbing, Lasts Hours to Days, >72 hr = Status Migrainosus)

  • Nausea/vomiting
  • Photophobia, phonophobia => sensitivity to light and sound
  • Rhinorrhea, lachrymation
  • Loss of appetite, fatigue

Postdromal Phase ("Migraine Hangover," Lasts 1–2 Days)

  • Pain with movement in migraine location
  • Exhaustion, dizziness
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Euphoria

Secondary Headaches

Manifestation Related Secondary Headache
Systemic manifestations (fever) Infection, nonvascular intracranial disorders, carcinoid, pheochromocytoma
Neoplasm in history Brain neoplasms, metastasis
Neurologic deficit or dysfunction Vascular/nonvascular intracranial disorders, brain abscess, infections
Sudden or abrupt headache onset Subarachnoid hemorrhage, cranial/cervical vascular disorders
Older age (>50 years) Giant cell arteritis, cranial/cervical vascular disorders, neoplasms, nonvascular intracranial disorders
Pattern change or recent onset Neoplasms, vascular/nonvascular intracranial disorders
Positional headache Intracranial hypertension or hypotension
Precipitated by sneezing, coughing, exercise Posterior fossa malformations, Chiari malformation
Papilledema Neoplasms, nonvascular intracranial disorders, intracranial hypertension
Progressive headache, atypical presentations Neoplasms, nonvascular intracranial disorders
Pregnancy or puerperium Cranial/cervical vascular disorders, postdural puncture headache, hypertension-related disorders (preeclampsia), cerebral sinus thrombosis, hypothyroidism, anemia, diabetes
Painful eye with autonomic features Pathology in posterior fossa, pituitary region, cavernous sinus, Tolosa-Hunt Syndrome, ophthalmic causes

Diagnostic

  • none; usually
  • MRI/CT - for older than 50 with no hx, if present with hypertension or AMS
  • Lumbar Puncture - analyze CSF for Meningitis

Treatment

  • Analgesia - usually OTC: aspirin, aceta, NSAID
  • Caffeine with analgesics
  • Opioids NOT recommended
  • Pharmacological treatments - only for headaches 3-15 times/month
  • Triptans - constrict intracranial vessels (e.g.: Sumatriptans, razatiptan) see Migraines pharm section
  • Anticonvulscants - valpiruc acid, topiramate
  • beta blockers
  • tricyclic antidepressants
  • botulinum toxins
  • IV fluids
  • steroids
  • non pharm: biofeedback, CBT, chiropractor, assage, craniosacal therapy, yoga, accupuntcture

Nursing Considerations

  • ensure dark, quiet room
  • NOTE: if patient complains that it is their WORST HEADACHE, assess for secondary headache to rule out life-threats

Diet

avoid: cheese, meats with nitrites, aspartame, pickled foods, MSG
