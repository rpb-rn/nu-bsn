Ventricular Tachycardia

  • Overview: A rapid, abnormal heart rhythm originating in the ventricles, often life-threatening and requiring immediate intervention.
  • Rate: 100-250 bpm
  • Rhythm: Regular
  • P-Waves: Not typically visible or unrelated to QRS complex
  • QRS Complex: Wide (greater than 0.12 seconds), bizarre shape
  • PR Interval: Not measurable or absent
  • Stable VTACH Treatment: minimal symptoms: administer amiodarone or lidocaine (antiarrhythmic drugs), consider cardioversion if drug therapy is ineffective.
  • unstable VTACH: immediate synchronized cardioversion or defibrillation if no pulse is present. Consider an ICD (Implantable Cardioverter Defibrillator) for recurrent episodes.
  • Symptoms: palpitations, syncope, chest pain, shortness of breath, cardiac arrest, or even sudden cardiac death.

Synchronized Cardioversion

  • typically less than what is required for defib
  • set to synchronize => track R waves
  • typically 50-100 joules, max 200

Remember: pulseless VT => defib, CPR, intubate, epi, ami/lido

note: VTACH is from ventricles, SVT is from atrium
note: VTACH has wide QRS, SVT has narrow QRS

Cardioversion - performed on clients who have a pulse but are not stable hemodynamically; a low energy, synchronous shock that is delivered at the exact point of the QRS complex - R to be exact

Note: follow CAB priority framework during CPR

Causes: underlying ischemic heart disease, acute MI, dilated cardiomyppathy, e-, structural heart disaese, illicit drug use (meth, coke)

Ventricular Fibrillation

  • Overview: A rapid, erratic electrical activity in the ventricles, causing ineffective quivering instead of a coordinated contraction, leading to no pulse and requiring immediate intervention.
  • Rate: Indeterminate (chaotic electrical activity), 150-500 bpm
  • Rhythm: Irregular, erratic
  • P-Waves: None
  • QRS Complex: None, appears as a chaotic, irregular baseline
  • PR Interval: None
  • Treatment: Immediate defibrillation is the primary treatment. Perform CPR until defibrillator is available, followed by epinephrine or amiodarone if necessary. Advanced cardiac life support (ACLS) protocols should be followed.

Causes: MI, structural heart disease, e- imbalance: hypomagnesemia, hypokalemia, hyperkalemia, hypoxia, acidosis, etc

Manifestations: sudden collapse due to cardiac arrest, no pulse, unresponsive

PEA - Pulseless Electrical Activity


  • Overview: A condition where there is organized electrical activity on the EKG but no mechanical heart activity or pulse, often associated with cardiac arrest, requiring immediate intervention.
  • Rate: Varies depending on underlying rhythm
  • Rhythm: Can be regular or irregular depending on underlying electrical activity
  • P-Waves: Present or absent, depending on the underlying rhythm
  • QRS Complex: Present or absent, depending on the underlying rhythm
  • PR Interval: Present or absent, depending on the underlying rhythm
  • Treatment: Immediate CPR, administer epinephrine, and correct the underlying cause (e.g., hypoxia, electrolyte imbalances, trauma). Follow ACLS protocols for resuscitation.

Primary Causes: cardiac
Secondary Causes: Hypoxia or resp failure (50% of cases), H's & T's

H: hypovolemia, hypoxia, htdrogen ion, hypo/per-kalemia, hypothermia
T: Tension pneumo, trauma, tamponade, thrombosis-pulm, thrombosis-coronary

NOTE: for CPR, follow CAB priority framework

  1. Epinephrine 1 mg q3-5 min
  2. amiodarone 300mg or lidocaine 1-1.5 mg/kg

Asystole

  • flatline
  • result of the failure of the intrinsic electrical system of the heart
  • often result of VFib, pulseless VTach, PEA
  • also be the result of attempted defibrillation for another dysrhythmia
  • terminal rhythm

Presentation: no pulse, unresponsive, flatline EKG

Treatment:

  • Asystole is a non-shockable rhythm!
  • intubation of the airway
  • CPR (with minimal interruption of < 5 seconds)
  • IV epinephrine (ideally within 5 minutes of the start of cardiac arrest) followed by 1 mg every 3 to 5 minutes.

First-Degree Heart Block

  • A conduction delay at the AV node resulting in prolonged PR interval without dropped beats; generally asymptomatic and often benign.
  • Rate: Usually normal
  • Rhythm: Regular
  • P-Waves: Present, one for each QRS complex
  • PR Interval: Prolonged (>0.20 seconds) but consistent
  • QRS Complex: Normal (0.06-0.10 seconds)
  • Treatment: Typically no treatment required unless symptomatic. Monitor for progression to higher-degree blocks. Address underlying causes such as medication effects, electrolyte imbalances, or ischemia.

Second-Degree Heart Block, Type I (Wenckebach)


Longer, longer, longer, drop! Then you have a WENCKEBACH

  • Overview: A progressive delay in AV node conduction until a QRS complex is dropped; often transient and may not require treatment.
  • Rate: Usually normal or slightly slow
  • Rhythm: Irregular due to dropped beats
  • P-Waves: Present, with more P-waves than QRS complexes
  • QRS Complex: Normal (0.06-0.10 seconds)
  • PR Interval: Progressively lengthens until a QRS complex is dropped
  • Treatment: Typically no intervention if asymptomatic. If symptomatic (e.g., dizziness, syncope), consider atropine (for brady) or temporary pacing. Monitor closely for progression to a higher-degree block.

Causes: medications sch as CCBs, beta-blockers, digitalis, amiodarone, adenosine, increase in vagal tone, post cardiac surgery, e- imbalance, cardiomyopathies, hyperkalemia, MI, myocarditis

Presentation: no physical manifestations, and minimal hemodynamic alterations

Second-Degree Heart Block, Type II

If some P’s don’t get through, then you have MOBITZ II

  • Overview: A more serious block where some P-waves are not conducted to the ventricles without a progressive PR interval change; higher risk of progressing to third-degree block.
  • Location: bundle of His, bundle branches, or fascicles (basically the ventricles)
  • Rate: Usually slow, depending on the number of dropped beats
  • Rhythm: Regular or irregular (based on conduction pattern)
  • P-Waves: Present, with more P-waves than QRS complexes
  • QRS Complex: Often widened (>0.12 seconds) if block occurs below the AV node
  • PR Interval: Constant in conducted beats (no progressive lengthening)
  • Treatment: Permanent pacemaker placement is often indicated due to risk of progression to complete heart block. Temporary pacing is done while waiting.

Other info:

  • Causes: MI, necrosis in heart, e-, CCBs, betablockers, amiodarone, digoxin, adenosine, post cardiac surgery
  • Patho: block in conduction system (usually bundle of HIS or bundle branches)
  • Presentation: bradycardia, fatigue, syncope, dyspnea, dizziness, palpitations, shortness of breath, and nausea

Things to note

  • if caused by meds, stop those meds
  • there is increased risk for falls d/t fatigue, dizziness, SOB

Third-Degree Heart Block

If P’s and Q’s don’t agree, then you have a THIRD DEGREE

  • Overview: A complete heartblock! Total disruption of electrical conduction between the atria and ventricles, causing them to beat independently; a medical emergency requiring immediate intervention.
  • Rate: Atrial rate typically normal, ventricular rate slow (usually < 40 bpm)
  • Rhythm: Regular, but atrial and ventricular rhythms are independent
  • P-Waves: Present, unrelated to QRS complexes (no consistent PR interval)
  • QRS Complex: May be narrow or wide depending on the location of the block
  • PR Interval: Variable with no consistent relationship between P-waves and QRS complexes
  • Treatment: Immediate intervention required; temporary transcutaneous pacing followed by permanent pacemaker placement is the primary treatment. Administer atropine or epinephrine if symptomatic while awaiting pacing

manifestations: varies: bradycardia, chest pain, shortness of breath, generalized fatigue, or syncope, altered LOC, JVD

bradycardic manifestations: decompensated heart failure, decreased capillary refill, cool skin, diaphoresis, altered mental status, tachypnea, cool skin, retraction, and respiratory distress

Things to note:

  • increased risk for falls
  • increased risk for nausea and aspiration
  • Atropine is for bradycardia since 3rd Degree blocks feature a ventricular escape rhythm (which is slower)
  • Amiodarone is for tachyarrhythmias (AFIB, VT, VF)

Pacemaker Considerations:

  • post-op: avoid strenuous activity, keep arm immobilized
  • do not drive for 2-3 weeks
  • use arm sling during night time (and during day if possible)
  • do not move arm above shoulder level
  • wear medical alert bracelet
  • beware of magnets, large motors, radars, MRIs
  • no contact sports
  • keep cellphones at least 6 inches
  • regular monitoring of function, battery life, identified abnormal rhythms
