Shock

Overview

Shock

  • life-threatening condition that occurs as a result of circulatory failure
  • causes decreased SVR and perfusion
  • leads to tissue hypoxia, cellular death, and organ dysfunction

4 Main Types:

  1. Distributive (septic, anaphylactic, neurogenic, toxic) => vasodilation
  2. Cardiogenic
  3. Hypovolemic
  4. Obstructive

Common Manifestations: tachycardia, tachypnea, hypotension, abnormal mental status, mottled skin, cold and clammy extremities, oliguria, hyperlactatemia, and metabolic acidosis

I. Distributive Shock

  • Systemic vasodilation
  • results in decreased perfusion the heart, brain, and kidneys => eventually organ damage
  • Capillary fluid leaks into the surrounding tissue causing further clinical deterioration with decreased systemic vascular resistance and abnormal oxygen utilization.
  • decreased CO, hypotension
  • Subtypes: septic, anaphylactic, neurogenic, toxic
  • risk factors: CKD, lots of lines, immunosuppression, recent surgery, old, cancer

Septic Shock

  • release of inflammatory cytokines in the presence of sepsis
  • cytokines damage blood vessel walls, triggering clotting and forming tiny clots
  • fluid leaks!
  • Causes: pancreatitis, burns, HAI
  • Treatment: abx within 1 hour, IVF (crystalloid - Salt solutions used for fluid replacement that can easily move around the body), norepi to manage BP
  • Resus: 3-mL/kg first 3 hours of resus

Anaphylactic Shock

  • severe hypersensitivity reaction that is mediated by IgE
  • results in the release of chemical mediators such as histamines, prostaglandins, leukotrienes
  • increases vascular permeability and subsequent decreased SVR, vasodilation, decreased perfusion to vital organs, and bronchoconstriction
  • Causes: exposure to an allergen (foods, latex, medications, animal venom, insect stings)
  • signature cue: exposure to an allergen such as medication, food, or insect sting
  • Manifestations: flushing, edema of the face and mouth, urticaria, hypotension, hoarseness, facial and oral edema, tachypnea, wheezing or stridor
  • FIRST LINE TX: IM EPI (give first before anything else), keep repeating until symptoms resolve => hold epi in muscle for 10 sec
  • remove cause of allergic rxn, IVF (crystalloids), corticosteroids, antihistamines, albuterol
  • Scenario: if giving abx & patient complains of itchiness and difficulty breathing, do the ff: 1. stop meds, 2. auscultate lungs, 3. epi

Neurogenic Shock

  • due to autonomic dysregulation caused by a primary and secondary spinal cord injury above the level of T6,
  • often associated with trauma to the cervical or thoracic vertebrae.
  • Primary injury: damage to axons, grey mater, and the anterior root
  • Secondary injury: causes electrolyte shifts, edema, and a vascular insult that leads to necrosis of the grey matter.
  • Causes: trauma, Guillain-Barre Syndrome, spinal anesthesia
  • Manifestations: brady cardia = unique symptom, flushed and warm skin, autonomic dysreflexia, temperature dysregulation, orthostatic hypotension, and bradyarrhythmias
  • Tx: IV NS ASAP

Toxic Shock

  • involves infection from Staphylococcus aureus and endotoxins from group A streptococcus
  • often resulting from the use of nasal or vaginal tampons
  • Other causes: burns, soft tissue or post-surgical infections, and dialysis catheters
  • manifestations: hypotension, rash resembling a sunburn, fever, and ultimately organ-failure related to vascular permeability.

II. Hypovolemic Shock

  • intravascular volume is decreased, resulting in poor CO
  • result of blood loss or extracellular fluid loss
  • 25-30% loss of volume
  • Hemorrhagic shock - insufficient perfusion of blood
  • inadequate O2 demands => anaerobic metabolism, by-product: lactic acid (leads to lactic acidosis => decreased cardiac contractility)
  • Coagulopathy: impaired coagulation as a result of blood loss
  • Signature cue: flattened neck veins and orthostatic hypotension
  • low central venous pressure (normal is 2-6 mmHg)
  • risk factors: trauma, Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm, PUD, maternal clients with bleeding risk
  • Nursing intervention: lower HOB (more perfusion to brain and core)
  • Note: infuse NS first before vasopressors (do not infuse vasopressors first!)
  • Put SPO2 on forehead instead of extremities for more accurate

Volume Loss Manifestations by %
<20% loss - anxiety, restlessness
20-40% loss - tachycardia, tachypnea, oliguria (dec. UOP), and orthostatic hypotension
40%+ loss: hypotension, severe tachycardia, and deteriorating mental status

Other manifestations: muscle cramps, thirst, abdominal or chest pain, lethargy, agitation, or confusion, decreased skin turgor, dry mucous membranes, tachycardia, cyanosis

Internal bleeding symptoms: hematemesis, bloody stool, and abdominal distention

Hemorrhagic Causes: trauma, rupture of esophageal varices GI bleed, aneurysm rupture, anticoags
Non-Hemorrhagic causes: excessive diuresis, burns, GI fluid loss, third spacing (ascites, edema, pleural effusion, interstitial shift)

Treatment: determine if hemorrhagic or not ASAP
Hemorrhagic: stop the bleed, administer blood products (PRBCs), FFP, plasma, large bor IV
Non-hemo: rapid infusion of warm isotonic crystalloids for a total of 30 mL/kg

do not give vasopressors in hypovolemic shock => it can worsen tissue perfusion (if given, infuse NS first)

III. Cardiogenic

  • Cardiac dysfunction causes myocardial ischemia => less CO => hypoperfusion
  • Compensatory mechanisms: peripheral vasoconstriction, tachycardia
  • Signature cues: chest pain and cardiac arrhythmias, narrowed pulse pressure, rhonchi, and distended jugular veins with elevated pulsations.
  • Causes: PE, metabolic acidosis, e- imbalance, cardiac nature
  • risk factors: 75 older, asian, pacific islander, female, DM, CVD, sepsis, obese
  • Signature manifestations: chest pain, arrhythmias, narrowed pulse pressure, ronchi, distented JV with pulsations
  • tx: goal is to restore optimal CO; vasopressors (norepi), inotropes (dobutamine, milrinone), IVF, treat cause or mechanical support
  • Positive Inotropic = more forceful beats, Double D's = Digoxin & Dopamine
  • Note: Dopamine can cause Tachycardia and Arrythmias (lower dose if this happens)

MCS: mechanical circulatory support. Examples: LVAD, balloon pump (IABP), ECMO, cardiac transplant

IV. Obstructive

  • blood flow is blocked and circulation to the major organs is disrupted
  • examples: pulmonary embolism, tension pneumo, mechanical ventilation with high levels of PEEP
  • other examples: aortic dissection, pericardiac tamponade, pericardiac effusion, mass within heart
  • other Causes: tension pneumo, ventilation high VEEP, PE
  • Risk factors: atherosclerotic disease, uncontrolled HTN, coagulopathies resulting in increased clotting, cancer, AIDS, pulmonary HTN, respiratory diseases or surgeries requiring the use of mechanical ventilation, and thoracic trauma.
  • Sx for Tension pneumo: diminished or absent breath sounds, tracheal deviation, or neck vein distention
  • sx for Cardiac Tamponade: Beck’s triad (muffled heart sounds, hypotension, increase jugular venous pressure) in addition to experiencing dyspnea.
  • sx for Pulmonary Embolism: pulsus paradoxus, hemoptysis
  • sx for Pericarditis, p. effusion: pulsus paradoxus
  • sx Aortic Dissection: pulse deficit
  • tx - remove obstruction, vasopressors

Pulmonary Embolism: signature sign: hymoptysis, pulsus paradoxus

Stages of Shock

  1. Initial Stage
    • Patho: Cellular hypoxia leads to anaerobic metabolism and lactic acid buildup
    • S/S: Often subtle; possible mild tachycardia and cool extremities
  2. Compensatory Stage
    • Patho: Body activates compensatory mechanisms (SNS activation, RAAS system) to maintain perfusion, "FIGHT OR FLIGHT", peripheral vasoconstriction
    • S/S: Tachycardia, increased respiratory rate, cool/clammy skin, decreased urine output, altered mental status.
  3. Progressive Stage
    • Patho: Compensatory mechanisms fail, resulting in worsening hypoxia and cellular dysfunction
    • S/S: Hypotension, weak pulses, severe confusion, anuria, metabolic acidosis, cold and clammy skin is priority in this stage
  4. Refractory Stage - irreversible
    • Patho: Widespread organ failure with severe hypoxia and cellular death,
    • S/S: Profound hypotension, unresponsive state, multiple organ dysfunction, death imminent

Other Stuff

Modified Early Warning Score (MEWS) - employed to identify risk of mortality and clinical deterioration based on SBP, RR, HR, mental status, and temperature.

Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT)

  • A dialysis method used in critically ill patients who are unable to tolerate traditional intermittent hemodialysis.
  • provides continuous, slow filtration of waste products and fluids from the blood.
  • may cause pneumothorax

Intra-Aortic Balloon Pump (IABP)

  • ballon inflates during diastole with aortic valve closure causing blood from aorta to flow into circulation better
  • treatment for cardiogenic shock
  • requires informed consent
  • inserted into femoral artery

Lactate Levels: elevated indicates hypoxia (normal is 0.5 to 2.0 mmol/L)

Nursing Tasks for Shock Patients

  • Monitor BP: must be at least 90 sys, 65 MAP, oliguria (dec. UOP), anuria (no UOP)
  • insert central lines, art lines, etc
  • IVF, PRBC, blood
  • trend HD
  • Manage IV drips: pressors (norepi), inotropes (dobutamine, milrinone)
  • nutrition or TPN
  • abx
  • indwelling urinary catheter
