HSV

What is it:

  • cold sores or fever blisters commonly found on lips
  • appear during certain vulnerable periods: stress, weakened immune system
  • main cause: skin to skin transmission

Risk Factors:

  • immunosuppressed, infants, young children, elevated stress
  • exposure to UV light
  • urban living
  • poorness

Manifestations

  • lesions with fluid filled pimples
  • eventually crust over, dry, and heal
  • malaise, sore throat, swollen lymph nodes, and other flu-like complaints
  • can also be asyptomatic

Complications: nutritional deficits from pain from eating
Diagnosis: exam, Viral culture, PCR testing, Serologic testing for HSV antibodies
Treatment: OTC creams, ibuprofen

Aphthous Ulcers

What is it

  • commonly known as a canker sore
  • circular, reddened lesion that most often presents with a white, gray, or yellowish center

Causes

  • not fully understood
  • probably bacterial
  • Risk Factors: more prevalent in areas with a greater proportion of middle- and high-income communities

Complications

  • nutritional deficits from pain from eating

Treatment

  • Topical analgesics (benzocaine, lidocaine)
  • Anti-inflammatory mouth rinses (steroid, chlorhexidine)
  • Nutritional supplementation if deficient
  • Severe cases: oral corticosteroids

Candidiasis

What is it

  • aka THRUSH;
  • fungal yeast infection
  • bright white patches or coatings that appear on the tongue and inside of the cheeks
    • other symptoms: irritation, redness, mouth pain (leads to eating difficulties)
    • can also be found in throat
  • occur in clients older than 65 years of age or in individuals who have a weakened immune system

Causes:

  • trauma to mouth

Risk Factors:

  • weakened immune system; 65 older
  • steroids and antibiotic use
  • stress
  • improperly fitting dentures
  • tobacco use

Diagnosis: history, biopsy

Gingivitis

What is it

  • Inflammation of the gums caused by plaque buildup along the gumline
    Causes

Manifestations

  • Red, swollen gums, bleeding with brushing or flossing, tenderness, bad breath
  • gum recession in severe cases

Diagnosis

  • Clinical examination, assessment of plaque levels, probing for gum pocket depth, and ruling out periodontal disease.

Treatment

  • Improved oral hygiene, professional dental cleanings
  • scaling to remove plaque/tartar
  • smoking cessation, and addressing underlying conditions.

Periodontitis

What is it

  • Chronic inflammatory gum disease causing destruction of soft tissue and bone supporting teeth

Causes

  • Bacterial plaque accumulation
  • Poor oral hygiene
  • Smoking, uncontrolled diabetes
  • Genetic predisposition

Risk Factors

  • Smoking, diabetes, poor oral hygiene
  • Immunosuppression, stress, hormonal changes
  • Aging, inadequate nutrition

Manifestations

  • Swollen, red, bleeding gums, gum recession, loose teeth, bad breath
  • Pain, pus between teeth and gums

Treatment

  • Scaling, root planing to remove plaque
  • Antibiotics (topical or systemic)
  • Surgery (flap surgery, bone grafts) in severe cases
  • Improved oral hygiene, smoking cessation

Nursing Considerations

  • Educate on proper oral care, regular dental visits
  • Assess for risk factors (smoking, diabetes)
  • Encourage smoking cessation, good nutrition
  • Monitor for complications like tooth loss, systemic infection

Oral Cancer

What is it: Malignant growth in oral cavity affecting lips, tongue, gums, cheeks, floor of mouth, or palate
Causes: tobacco use (smoking, chewing), Excessive alcohol consumption, HPV infection, Chronic irritation (ill-fitting dentures, sharp teeth)
Risk Factors

  • male
  • chewing/smoking tobacco
  • alcohol consumption
  • poor oral hygiene
  • UV light exposure
  • HPV
  • candida infections
  • immunosuppression
  • chronic inflammation of mount

Manifestations

  • Non-healing ulcers, red/white patches
  • Pain, bleeding, difficulty chewing/swallowing
  • Lump, thickening, numbness in mouth
  • Hoarseness, ear pain, weight loss in advanced cases

Diagnosis: direct pharyngoscopy and laryngoscopy, biopsy, scraping of tongue with diagnostic dye

Treatment:

  • Surgery (tumor removal, possible reconstruction)
  • Radiation, chemotherapy for advanced cases
  • Targeted therapy, immunotherapy (specific cases)
