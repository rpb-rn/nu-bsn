Pancreatitis

#nursing #medsurg #gastrointestinal

What is it

  • inflammation of the pancreas (from autodigestion of pancreas = digestive enzymes eat the pancreas)
  • stems from pancreatic duct obstruction (see causes)
  • acute or chronic
  • enzymes are key to understanding this disease: protease, lipase, amylase
  • also causes Hepatitis symptoms due to blockage of common bile duct (liver and pancreas shares)

Pancreas: produces enzymes to digest food (note carbs, fats, proteins); releases insulin and glucagon to regular blood sugar

Acute Pancreatitis - sudden, releases trypsin that causes pancreatic acinar cell death = inflammatory response, usually managed with conservative treatments
Chronic Pancreatitis - ongoing, progressive inflammation, may cause irreversible organ damage from fibrosis and scarring

Causes

  • gall stones and alcohol abuse = most common cause
  • gall stones - obstruct the pancreatic duct (see Cholecystitis); obstruction causes enzymes to autodigest
  • alcohol: constant inflammation occludes the duct
  • diabetes & obesity
  • Post-ERCP
  • abdominal surgery
  • trauma
  • infection

Risk Factors

  • alcohol abuse
  • smoking
  • high-fat diet which leads to gallstone formation
  • family hx, obesity, DM, cystic fibrosis
  • old

Clinical Manifestations

  • intense pain in epigastric area or left upper abd (radiates to back, worsens after eating)
  • abdominal tenderness with guarding
  • Hypotension - third spacing (fluid shift to interstitial space), SIRS, E- imbalance, sepsis
  • Cullen's Sign - blue grey discoloration sign around belly button (Edward Cullen is blue-grey)
  • Grey-Turner's Sign - ecchymosis on the patient's flank (you turn to the side to see it)
  • Fox's Sign - ecchymosis on groin (fox = fuck area / groin)
  • Infection: fever, chills, tachy, diaphoresis, tachypnea
  • N/V
  • Jaundice (see Hepatitis) => pancreas and bile duct share a common pathway into duodenum, gallstones can also block the common bile duct
  • Dark Urine - tell-tale sign of elevated bilirubin levels
  • clay-colored stools
  • also: oily stool (steatorrhea)
  • pruritus (from bile salt accumulation)
  • Tetany - involuntary muscle contractions, typically caused by low levels of calcium in the blood (hypocalcemia)
  • dehydration

Complications

  • AKI or CKD
  • necrotizing pancreatitis - tissue death
  • Pancreatic pseudocysts (fluid-filled sacs, can rupture and cause bleeding)
  • SIRS => MODS - greatest risk for death if unresolved within 48 hrs
  • sepsis
  • malnutrition
  • Diabetes T2
  • Pancreatic Cancer
  • Recurrent pancreatitis - once you get it, you will most likely get it again
  • ARDS (yes)

Diagnosis

  • Lipase: elevated, best marker detecting Pancreatitis due to its specificity (lipase is an enzyme that breaks down FATS, secreted by pancreas thru bile salts)
  • lipase levels: 0-160 u/L
  • Amylase: elevated, also used but can also be elevated in Bowel Obstruction, Gastric Ulcers (Amylase is an enzyme that breaks down CARBS, secreted by pancreas thru bile salts)
  • WBC = elevated (see CBC)
  • Bilirubin = elevated (see LFT)
  • CMP: low Ca
  • Glucose (pancreas can't make insulin) = increased
  • Imaging: US (preferred to detect gallstone), CT, MRCP, ERCP
  • CRP/ESR = elevated

Treatment

  • reduce inflammation first, then treat the cause
  • strict NPO with I/O recording (rest pancreas, reduce enzyme secretion)
  • IVF for e- correction
  • NGT nutrition or TPN or PRN decompression
  • Analgesia
  • progress to clear liquid, bland diet, low fat diet as tolerated
  • If blockage is confirmed = ERCP (A scope gets shoved down your throat to check your liver, gallbladder, and pancreas, can also remove gallstones)
  • Cholecystectomy
  • Drainage of pancreas if indicated
  • For chronic pancreatitis: Enzyme supplements

Diet (if not NPO)

  • Low FAT
  • Low SUGARS
  • provide enzymes with food, if prescribed

NOTE: once pancreatitis resolves, amylase will return to normal within 3-6 hours! Lipase will remain elevated for 14 days!
